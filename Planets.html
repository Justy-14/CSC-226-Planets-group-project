System Archives

Mercury & Venus

These inner planets show dramatic phase changes throughout the year, similar to the Moon. Mercury can appear as a thin crescent or nearly full, while Venus becomes visibly gibbous even to the naked eye.

Outer Planets

The outer planets (Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune) show minimal phase variations because they orbit beyond Earth. We see nearly the entire illuminated side year-round, with phase angles rarely exceeding a few degrees.

The Artemis II Earthrise

During the historic Artemis II mission, four astronauts became the first humans to fly around the Moon in over 50 years. As their Orion spacecraft looped behind the lunar far side, they witnessed a breathtaking "Earthrise"—seeing our planet rise over the cratered horizon as a partially illuminated phase.

Galileo & The Phases of Venus

When Galileo Galilei first pointed his telescope at Venus in 1610, he noticed it went through a full set of phases just like our Moon. This groundbreaking discovery proved that Venus orbits the Sun, not the Earth, delivering the final blow to the idea that Earth was the center of the universe.

The "Da Vinci Glow"

Have you ever looked at a thin crescent Moon and noticed that the rest of the dark circle is faintly glowing? This phenomenon, called Earthshine or the "Da Vinci glow," is caused by sunlight reflecting off Earth's highly reflective oceans and clouds, bouncing onto the Moon, and reflecting back to our eyes.
